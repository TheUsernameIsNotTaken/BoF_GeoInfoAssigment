Historical Context.

Artillery Analysis at the Battle of Fredericksburg by Professor Peter Guth

Introduction

The Battle of Fredericksburg was fought between General Robert E. Lee´s Confederate Army of Northern Virginia and the Major General Ambrose E. Burnside´s Union Army of the Potomac. It is remembered as one of the most one-sided battles of the American Civil War in which the Union Army suffered terrible casualties in frontal assaults on entrenched Confederate defenders. It is also remembered as one of the most significant artillery engagements of the war, which is the driving force behind the analysis.

Target Audience

The audience for this project could really be anyone from someone just curious about the battle to a student doing a research project on the effectiveness of artillery in battle. The visuals in the project give someone that is just taking a glance a good impression of how effective artillery was used. On the other hand, if someone is doing research on the battle, they can click on the individual batteries to get a picture and more detail.

Process

Using Microdem and other GIS resources I was able to take a historic map of the battlefield and register it´s location on Google Earth. The map was originally a jpg2000 file, which was converted to a PNG file which had to be registered. On the same map I put an overlay of major roads, streams and railroads using TIGER files from counties Stafford, Spotsylvania and Fredericksburg.

                                    

Using a DEM of the area, I was able to make weapon fans the artillery and make a visual representation of what each battery had the capability of targeting. This was done for both the Confederate and the Union artillery batteries. After the weapon fans were completed and adjusted, I made a database of it, which was placed onto the historic map.

                                   

This final composite of maps, databases and other information gives a good visual representation of what each army was able to target using their artillery batteries and why this battle is considered to be a major artillery engagement.

Summary

The entire project was very difficult to start. I had a very difficult time finding the required information on the batteries and type of artillery used at each battery. It was obvious that the Battle of Fredericksburg has a lack of historical information on it compared to more famous battles such as Antietam and Gettysburg. I also had a very difficult time registering my historic map. There was no coordinate system on my historic map which resulted in me having to try and register notable landmarks and intersections. The accuracy of the registered map compared to the real world is average at best, but for purposes of the artillery analysis it is still usable. I learned a great deal from this project, not only on the Battle of Fredericksburg, but the many capabilities of Microdem and the power that GIS contains.

Metadata

NED: The National Elevation Dataset from seamless.usgs.gov.

TIGER: Data for Fredericksburg, Stafford and Spotsylvania counties gathered from http://www2.census.gov/geo/tiger/tiger2007.

Historic Map: The Library of Congress at http://memory.loc.gov/ammem/index.html.

User Guide.

When you open the project first, you will find a Leaflet map showing historical data about the battle of Fredericksburg. You will find a layer selector on the top right corner of the map. Hover over it to change base layer, or to select between which overlay layer(s) to show.

Change Base Layer

You can choose between OpenStreetMap(seen as OSM on the layer selector), MapBox's streets (seen as MapBox street on the layer selector) and MapBox's satelite(seen as Satelite on the layer selector). The default base layer is the Satelite layer. Choose one to set the base layer to an other one.

Choose Overlay Layer

You have four overlay layer to choose from, three of them being active at the start.

Choosing one of the "Confederate/Union Weapons fans" layers, will show the choosen side's artillery firing position's firing range with green, and will also shown a historical topographic and hydrographic map of the battlefield. You can choose to view only one of them at a time, and compare the historical and current landscape, or view both of them, and compare the two sides artillery support zones that way.

Selecting or deselecting the "Union/Confederate Arillery units" layers enable or disable the artilerry unit's markers in the map. Hover over these markers to see who commended which artillery unit during the battle, and click them to see what kind of artillery was used on which position.

Technical Documentation.

The Project's map was created using Leaflet. The map's size is dinamically changing, trying to fill the available space. It has imageOverlay layers, geoJSON layers and generated markers by GeoJSON data. The database was in kmz, so I saved it into kml, and converted it into GeoJSON with an online converter. After convertion I needed ot adjust some feature data (like fixing the image links, but it was done easily and fast with an usage of a replace all command).

Base Layers

The baseLayers are loaded into tileLayer-s. The three if them added into a contlor layer as it's baseLayer-s. I used the codes and methods learned during the labor classes.

Overlay Layer

I set the three overlay layers to be active, by adding them into the map by either during map declaration or after, in at the end of GeoJSON loading process.

The "Confederate/Union Weapons fans" layers are both an imageOverlay layer using pre-defined coordinates and puctures from the database.

The "Union/Confederate Arillery units" layers are loaded from two GeoJSON files, using fetch. I used the "pointToLayer" option to create an marker with a defined custom icon, for every point feature in the datasets. I also used the "onEachFeature" option to generate a custom tooltip and popup for each marker, using the feature's metadata. This popup contains the feature's name, it's image and it's description, which contains the artillery's type. The tooltip only contains the feature's name, and it's "only show when hovered over" behavior is thanks to it's "permanent:false" option. More info about the used options.
